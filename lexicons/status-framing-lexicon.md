# Status Framing Lexicon

Lexicon for shaping how status updates are communicated to control perception, reduce unnecessary concern, and minimize follow-up questions.

## Balanced Status Updates (Low-Panic, Low-Follow-Up)

- Instead of: My coworker put in their notice and I am scrambling to reassign work, verify responsibilities, and handle knowledge transfer

- Use: This week’s planned tasks have been moved to next week due to recent team adjustments that required a short-term reprioritization

- Context: Use when timelines shift due to internal changes and the goal is to provide enough context to prevent follow-up questions without introducing concern

- Notes: “recent team adjustments” signals a real cause without implying disruption; “short-term reprioritization” reinforces control and containment

## Preferred Alternatives to "Align" / "Alignment"

Avoid overusing `align`, `alignment`, and `get on the same page` when a more direct phrase would be clearer and more natural.

### Prefer

- `clarify` for ambiguity
- `confirm` for established direction
- `agree on` for joint decisions
- `settle on` for choosing between options
- `talk through` for collaborative discussion
- `shared understanding` for polished written communication

### Examples

- Instead of: align on priorities  
  Use: confirm priorities / agree on priorities

- Instead of: align on next steps  
  Use: confirm next steps / settle on next steps

- Instead of: align on scope  
  Use: clarify the scope / confirm scope boundaries

- Instead of: get on the same page  
  Use: make sure we have the same understanding / talk it through

### Notes

Prefer direct, human wording over abstract business phrasing. Use the simplest phrase that accurately reflects the communication goal.
